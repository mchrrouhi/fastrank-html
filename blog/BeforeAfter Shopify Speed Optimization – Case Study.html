Guides and checklists are useful, but sometimes you just want to see a real example of what happens when a slow Shopify store goes through professional optimization. That’s what this before/after case study is about.

We’ll keep the client anonymous and call them Northpeak Gear — a mid-sized Shopify Plus store selling premium outdoor equipment in Europe and North America. They came to us after noticing that new product launches were underperforming, despite strong creative and competitive pricing.

1. Store background & performance problems

Northpeak Gear had a solid brand and a loyal audience. Their Meta and Google Ads campaigns were well structured, their creatives tested well, and their AOV was healthy. Yet, performance had been trending in the wrong direction for months:

  • Mobile conversion rate had dropped from 2.9% to 2.1% year over year.
  • Cost per purchase on Meta had climbed by nearly 30%.
  • Google Search Console was showing Core Web Vitals warnings on key product templates.

Internally, the team knew the store felt heavy. Over the years they had added:

  • Multiple review, UGC and social proof apps.
  • Several pop-up and announcement bar tools.
  • Three different tracking/analytics suites.
  • A custom theme built on top of an older Shopify framework.

They had tried a few “one-click” optimization apps, but nothing produced lasting improvements. That’s when they reached out for a structured Shopify speed optimization service instead of guessing with settings.

2. Baseline metrics and Core Web Vitals

Every FASTRANK project starts with an audit. For this store, we focused on three critical templates: the homepage, a best-selling product page, and a high-traffic collection page.

Baseline mobile metrics (product page):

  • PageSpeed Insights mobile score: 41/100
  • Largest Contentful Paint (LCP): 5.8s
  • Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS): 0.21
  • Interaction to Next Paint (INP): 380ms

From a business perspective, the team reported:

  • Cart abandonment especially high on mobile.
  • Frequent “site feels slow” comments in support tickets.
  • Meta showing “landing page experience: below average” for key ad sets.

With the baseline in place, we moved into a structured optimization flow — the same one we describe visually in our slow store checklist article.

3. Step 1 – App & script cleanup

The first step was to address app bloat. Over time, the store had accumulated more than 25 installed apps, many of which injected scripts on every page.

  • Removed 8 unused apps: legacy countdown timers, an abandoned A/B testing toolkit, and a rarely used chat widget.
  • Consolidated social proof: replaced two overlapping review/UGC apps with a single lightweight solution.
  • Rationalized tracking: removed duplicate Facebook pixels and an outdated analytics integration that was no longer needed.
  • Cleaned leftover code: on a duplicate theme, we carefully removed script tags and snippets left behind in theme.liquid and section files by uninstalled apps.

This alone cut dozens of network requests from the initial page load and reduced total JavaScript size by roughly 35%.

⚠ Important: App cleanup isn’t just about deletion. Many apps continue to load scripts even after you disable them in the admin panel. You need to remove their leftover code from the theme, which is why we always work on a staging copy first.

4. Step 2 – Theme & layout optimization

With the worst app issues handled, we focused on the theme itself — how HTML, CSS and layout choices were affecting LCP, CLS and INP.

  • Reordered critical content: ensured the hero product image and key above-the-fold content were requested early in the HTML.
  • Reduced layout complexity: simplified nested sections on the product template and removed a heavy slider module on mobile.
  • Reserved space for key elements: added explicit height values for the hero image, announcement bar and review widget to prevent layout jumps.
  • Improved navigation behavior: refactored the mobile menu to be lighter and more responsive, which helped INP on tap events.

The goal wasn’t to change the visual brand; it was to make the existing layout behave predictably and load intelligently, especially for mobile visitors on slower networks.

5. Step 3 – Image & media fixes

The next obvious bottleneck was imagery. Like many merchants, Northpeak uploaded high-resolution product photos straight from the camera and relied on Shopify to resize them.

  • Compressed hero images: reduced key banners from 1.5–2MB down to ~200KB with no visible loss in quality.
  • Introduced WebP versions: served modern formats to supported browsers while keeping safe fallbacks.
  • Standardized aspect ratios: aligned product card thumbnails to a consistent ratio, which also improved CLS on collection pages.
  • Enabled lazy loading: below-the-fold product galleries and lifestyle images now load only when scrolled into view.

These changes dramatically reduced how long it took for the primary product media (the LCP element on most templates) to become visible.

6. Step 4 – Core Web Vitals tuning

With bloat reduced and layout stabilized, we moved into precise Core Web Vitals tuning. This is where the project switched from “general speed work” to more advanced Shopify performance optimization.

  • Deferred non-critical scripts: many marketing and tracking scripts were moved to load after the first paint, reducing main-thread work at the moment of initial interaction.
  • Optimized fonts: limited the number of font weights and enabled font-display strategies to avoid invisible text and layout shifts.
  • Reduced main-thread blocking: refactored a heavy custom script controlling variant swatches so it only runs when needed.
  • Set tighter cache rules: leveraged Shopify’s infrastructure alongside a CDN to improve repeat visit performance.

At this stage we also set up ongoing monitoring, so the team could see how their real-user Core Web Vitals evolved in the weeks after deployment.

7. Before/after results (scores & revenue)

After deploying the optimized theme and letting data accumulate, we compared the new results with the baseline.

Updated mobile metrics (product page):

  • PageSpeed Insights mobile score: 41 → 91
  • Largest Contentful Paint (LCP): 5.8s → 1.9s
  • Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS): 0.21 → 0.03
  • Interaction to Next Paint (INP): 380ms → 120ms

Business impact over the following 8 weeks:

  • Mobile conversion rate improved by 27%.
  • Average pages per session increased by 18%.
  • Meta reported “landing page experience: above average” across core ad sets.
  • Cost per purchase on paid traffic dropped by roughly 15–20%, depending on campaign.

From the client’s perspective, the most noticeable change was qualitative: “The store finally feels snappy again.” Product pages no longer hesitated when opening, and elements stayed where shoppers expected them to be.

8. Key lessons for other Shopify merchants

While every store is unique, several lessons from this project apply broadly to most Shopify setups:

  • Speed issues are usually cumulative. No single app or image “breaks” performance; it’s the combination of years of small decisions.
  • Core Web Vitals react well to structured work. When you follow a methodical checklist, improvements in LCP, CLS and INP tend to be predictable.
  • Design rarely needs a full rebuild. In this case, the store kept its overall aesthetic. We optimized how assets loaded, not the brand itself.
  • Monitoring matters. Once you fix a slow store, you need guardrails so future changes don’t silently drag performance down again.
